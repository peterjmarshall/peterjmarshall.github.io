Research Focus

Psychology and neuroscience have largely developed in parallel with biology rather than in genuine contact with it. Genes are invoked as causes without engaging what developmental biology has learned about how genes actually work. The brain is studied as an isolated processor without engaging what evolutionary biology tells us about why brains exist. Development is treated as background context rather than as a central explanatory framework.

My research takes a different starting point. Living things are not passive products of genes and environments. They are self-organizing beings that actively participate in their own becoming, enacting worlds through their activity within constraints that both limit and enable what can emerge. The organization through which an organism perceives, acts, and makes meaning is not encoded anywhere in advance. It arises through development, a process the organism is part of from the start.

This requires bringing together perspectives that are rarely in conversation: developmental biology, evolutionary biology, embodied cognitive science, and developmental systems theory. The goal is a more coherent account of mind, brain, and development that takes the biology seriously without falling back on the old pictures.

About This Project

This project grew out of a conviction that students of psychology and neuroscience deserve a richer picture of what living things are and how they develop. Standard courses tend to frame development as the unfolding of a genetic blueprint, or the mind as a passive processor of environmental inputs. Neither picture holds up, and neither prepares students to think seriously about life, mind, or behavior.

The three OER sites make these ideas accessible to students. A book developing the academic argument is in progress. The sites and the book are separate efforts, but they share the same starting point: that getting the biology right is not a matter of adding more neuroscience to psychology, but of rethinking what kind of thing a living, developing organism is.

This project is still evolving.
