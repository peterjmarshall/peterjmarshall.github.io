Peter J. Marshall

Toward a biologically coherent account of living, thinking, and developing.

Professor of Psychology and Neuroscience at Temple University in Philadelphia. I am a developmental psychologist with a background in biology, working on bringing psychology and neuroscience into closer contact with what biology is actually about. The biology that tends to inform both fields is a simplified picture, one in which genes cause traits, selection optimizes them, and development executes the plan. What biology is actually about is considerably more interesting, and the gap has real consequences for how we understand mind, brain, and behavior.

The Problem

There are converging currents in biology that psychology has largely not engaged with. The mainstream of evolutionary biology remains committed to a gene-centric picture, and most psychologists who invoke biology are drawing on that picture: genes cause traits, selection optimizes them, development executes the plan. But at the edges of developmental biology, evolutionary theory, and the study of living systems, a different account has been taking shape. Developmental systems theory, work on self-organization and physical morphogenesis, and enactive approaches to cognition converge on something the standard picture cannot accommodate: the question of how reliable, species-typical features of development come about at all. The usual answer is that they were built in by evolution, inherited, there from the start. But this gets things backwards. The stability we see in development is not simply received. It is produced, through the active participation of the organism in constructing the very conditions that shape its own development.

What a more current biology shows is that the complexity of a developing brain cannot be read off from a genome. Reliable development requires the faithful reconstruction, across generations, of a system of constraints involving cells, physical forces, molecular gradients, self-generated activity, and organism-environment coupling. That system has no master molecule doing the directing. Form arises through the process, not before it. Getting that right changes what questions are worth asking about mind, brain, and behavior.

The Work

Development is not just something that happens to infants and children. It is a feature of life itself, operating across timescales from the evolution of life cycles to the moment-to-moment activity of an organism in its world. Putting development at the center changes what biology is about and what psychology needs to explain.

Understanding development requires a clearer account of what an individual organism actually is. Living things are not passive vehicles for genetic programs. They are self-organizing, self-maintaining beings that actively participate in their own becoming, enacting worlds through their engagement with their surroundings. What makes living things different from non-living things is not a special ingredient but a particular kind of organization, one that arises through development and is sustained through activity.

In humans, that picture becomes more complex. Brain development, evolutionary history, and cultural transmission are caught in feedback loops that cannot be pulled apart. Extended childhood, social learning, the intergenerational reconstruction of practices and environments: these are not additions to a biological story. They are part of it.

This Project

This project grew out of a conviction that students of psychology and neuroscience deserve a richer picture of what living things are and how they develop. Standard courses tend to frame development as the unfolding of a genetic blueprint, or the mind as a passive processor of environmental inputs. Neither picture holds up, and neither prepares students to think seriously about life, mind, or behavior.

The three sites are open educational resources, free to use and still evolving. Together they are an attempt to build materials that connect developmental biology, psychology, and neuroscience into a more coherent account of what it means to be a living, thinking, developing being.
