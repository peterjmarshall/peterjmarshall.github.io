Embodiment, Development, and Neuroscience

About My Research

My research investigates the development of neural body representations and their role in social cognition across the lifespan. Using EEG and other neuroscience methods, my lab examines how the brain comes to represent both our own bodies and those of others, particularly during infancy and early childhood. This work connects to fundamental questions about embodied cognition - how our physical experiences shape the way we think and learn. Recently, my research has expanded in two key directions: studying human-robot interaction through the lens of embodiment, with a focus on how children perceive and engage with social robots; and developing theoretical frameworks that can help advance a more biologically coherent understanding of brain development. This integrative approach brings together insights from developmental science, neuroscience, and embodied cognition to better understand how humans develop the ability to connect with others through shared bodily experiences. My work has been funded by major grants from the National Science Foundation, National Institutes of Health, and the James S. McDonnell Foundation.

Open Educational Resources

  • About Development
    This collection of interconnected essays explores fundamental questions about development, form, and change across biological and psychological domains. The materials examine how individual development occurs within broader patterns of life cycles and evolution, drawing on insights from developmental systems theory, evolutionary developmental biology, and cultural-biological approaches. Key themes include the relationship between form and matter, the nature of pattern explanation, and how stable developmental trajectories emerge without rigid predetermination. The resources challenge conventional genetic determinism while offering new ways to understand the reliability of developmental processes. Written to be accessible yet rigorous, these materials provide an integrated framework for understanding development that bridges multiple explanatory frames - from immediate mechanisms to evolutionary time scales. The collection serves as both an introduction to developmental thinking and a deeper exploration of how forms emerge and persist in living systems.
  • Enacting Life
    These educational materials explore how living things create meaning through their active engagement with the world. Drawing from research across biology, cognitive science, and philosophy, the resources examine how different organisms - from bacteria to humans - bring forth their own unique perspectives and ways of being. Rather than passive information processors, living things actively construct their realities through their embodied activities and interactions with their environments. The materials cover key concepts like autopoiesis (self-creation), umwelt (self-world), structural coupling between organisms and environments, and the deep connections between biological and cultural evolution. Written for a broad audience, these resources aim to illuminate the profound ways that life shapes experience and meaning-making across multiple scales - from the cellular to the social. The goal is to help readers appreciate both the diversity of lived perspectives in nature and the common patterns that unite all living things as meaning-makers.
  • Human Brains
    This open educational resource explores fundamental questions about human development through an integrative lens that connects evolutionary, developmental, and cultural perspectives. The materials examine how brains, bodies, and minds develop in the context of human experience, moving beyond simple nature-versus-nurture debates to understand development as a dynamic process. Key themes include the embodied nature of cognition, the role of social interaction in human development, the cultural-biological nature of human experience, and how developmental systems theory helps us understand both stability and change across the lifespan. The resources integrate insights from biology, psychology, anthropology and philosophy to provide students with a rich, multidimensional understanding of human development.
